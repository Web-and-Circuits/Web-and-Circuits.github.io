Weight, push, and angle change the amplitude (how far it swings) but not the period (how long each swing takes). This surprises nearly everyone. Galileo supposedly discovered it watching a chandelier in the Pisa cathedral.

Duckworth's point: if someone told you "only length matters," you'd nod and forget. By experimenting, you now own that knowledge. "Having confidence in one's ideas does not mean 'I know my ideas are right'; it means 'I am willing to try out my ideas.'"

V. The Clinical Interview

The "wrong" answer is the interesting one.

A child gives a "wrong" answer. Your instinct: correct them. Duckworth's method: ask why. Discover the sophisticated reasoning behind the "error."

VI. Give Them Time

Rushing kills wonderful ideas.

"I needed time for my confusion."

Same puzzle. Two conditions. Notice how your thinking changes.

With a timer, you likely guessed or panicked. Without one, you could think.

Duckworth's thesis: "All of us need time for our confusion if we are to build the breadth and depth that give significance to our knowledge." Speed tests measure recall, not understanding. The most powerful ideas come to people who are given time to sit with not-knowing.

Piaget's answer to "the American question" (How do we speed up development?): "The question is not how fast you go but how far you go."

VII. The Ramp Dots

Predict first. Then see.

A ball rolls down a ramp, leaving paint dots at regular time intervals. Where are the dots closest together? Where farthest apart?

Make your prediction:

Closer at bottom

Faster = more dots per area

Farther at bottom

Faster = more distance between dots

Evenly spaced

Rotation is constant

Not sure

I'd need to see it

The dots get farther apart toward the bottom. The ball drops paint at regular time intervals, but covers more distance each interval as it accelerates.

One teacher, seeing the result, measured with a string and said: "They don't get closer together as noticeably as I thought they would!" She was still seeing what she expected, even with the evidence in front of her.

That confusion is productive. Sit with being wrong. The gap between your prediction and reality is where learning lives.
