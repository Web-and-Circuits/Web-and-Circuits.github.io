This is not a conservation book. It's not a travel book. It's Douglas Adams applying the full force of one of the finest comic minds of the twentieth century to the actual, physical world ‚Äî and discovering that reality is funnier, stranger, and more heartbreaking than anything he ever invented for The Hitchhiker's Guide.

Adams approaches everything as a curious outsider, not an expert. He finds the absurd angle that reveals truth better than straight reporting ever could. The digressions are the point ‚Äî his tangent about airports, about Dr. Struan Sutherland's fairy cakes and hydroponics, about the utter impossibility of buying condoms in Shanghai ‚Äî these are not decorations on the argument, they are the argument. The systems that make extinction possible are the same systems that make airports terrible. It's all connected.

Every chapter lands a different emotional payload. The Komodo dragon chapter is about horror and our reflexive need to project evil onto creatures that know nothing of it. The gorilla chapter is about kinship across an evolutionary gulf. The kakapo chapter is about the gentle absurdity of a creature so perfectly adapted to safety that it has forgotten danger exists. And Adams never preaches. He makes you laugh, then you realise you're heartbroken.
