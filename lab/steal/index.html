üìñ The Review

‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÖ‚òÜ‚òÜ ‚Äî Charming Gateway Drug

This book is likeable but lightweight. It's Instagram wisdom before Instagram wisdom was a genre. The ideas are real ‚Äî influence genealogy, constraint-as-liberation, process-over-product ‚Äî but none are developed past the aphorism stage.

Kleon spends 2 pages on "use your hands" where Papert spends 20 on the epistemology of turtle geometry. The book says in 150 pages what could be (and originally was) a blog post turned into a talk at Broome Community College.

But here's the thing: it works as a gateway. Someone reads this at 19, gets fired up, and eventually finds the deeper books. The Good Theft / Bad Theft framework is genuinely useful as a creative ethics checklist. And "our failure to copy our heroes is where we discover where our own thing lives" is a legitimately beautiful sentence.

Best Contribution

The influence genealogy idea ‚Äî that you actively choose your creative ancestors the way you can't choose your biological ones ‚Äî is the book's most fertile concept. It reframes "originality anxiety" as something productive: you're not stealing, you're building a family tree.

Connections to Other Books

  • Mindstorms (Papert) ‚Äî Both argue for learning-by-doing, but Papert has 100x the theoretical depth. Kleon's "fake it til you make it" is a folk version of Piagetian learning.
  • How to Solve It (P√≥lya) ‚Äî Both emphasize process over product. P√≥lya's heuristics are the rigorous version of Kleon's "steal the thinking behind the style."
  • Inmates (Cooper) ‚Äî Kleon's "two desks" echoes Cooper's separation of design from implementation.
  • TAOUP (Raymond) ‚Äî Both celebrate the remix/recombination ethos. "Good artists borrow, great artists steal" comes from the same tradition.
  • Vehicles (Braitenberg) ‚Äî The genealogy of ideas is structurally similar to how Braitenberg builds complex behavior from simple parts ‚Äî ideas combine and produce emergent originality.

The Honest Take

If you've read widely in creativity, design, or education, you already know everything in this book. It's a well-designed sampler plate of ideas that exist in much richer form elsewhere.

But Kleon would probably be fine with that assessment. After all ‚Äî he's the one who said nothing is original.
